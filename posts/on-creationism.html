Natural selection

On the contrary to what the movie claims, with organisms that have short life spans and thus fast reproduction rates, the evolution into a separate specie has been observed. In 1975 a nylon-eating bacteria has been discovered that must have evolved within 40 years from invention of nylon in 1935.

On top of that, during mutation genes can be duplicated thus increasing the amount on information encoded in DNA. To recap: mutations do not require external force, and they can make the individual better or worse adapt to an environment, even if the change appear to be negative on the first look.

fossil remains supporting evolution

According to the theory [of evolution] the origination of such diverse living beings took place through minor and successive variations over long period of times.

That is what Darwin claimed and is only one possibility. Another is presented by Punctuated equilibrium which states that for the most part, species will not change much, but when rapid change occur in environment, organism evolve rapidly.

Imagine an animal which eats tree leafs. Times are good and there is a lot of trees so it does not have problems obtaining food. Every now and than, an individual with unusually long neck is born, but because there is plenty of food, there is no advantage in reaching leafs high on the tree. Because of this, natural selection does not favour those individuals and their genes mix with the rest of the species so that average neck length of the whole population stays relatively constant.

However, if a sudden environmental change (for instance climate change) causes most of the trees to die, suddenly long neck is a desired trait. Natural selection starts favouring individuals with long necks and the specie evolves until a state of equilibrium is reached again.

Moreover, there is no difference between fossils hundreds of millions of years and their modern descendants.

Again, Show me the intermediate fossils shows fossil records in which the difference is clearly seen and reasonably gradual. The fact that some species do not change much is not inconsistent with the theory — if given specie is well adapted to the environment, there is no need for change (if it’s not broken, don’t fix it).

I’m an atheist, but I’ve always found somewhat weird the anti-evolution arguments from creationists along the lines of "XYZ is too unlikely, it’s impossible to occur naturally, must have been hand-crafted by God". Ironically that goes against the notion of omnipotence-"ominintelligence"-omniscience of the God/designer. When I was a theist/deist I just thought that God would just have created a universe that spontaneously develops in the way it apparently did, according to what is observed by science. Even if our ultimate origin is not a "likely" event, it could still be certain, if omniscience is on the table. If one believes in an omnipotent creator God, one can’t say something natural is absolutely "impossible". The actual theory can still be questioned as unlikely, but you’d not do the argument that is equivalent to saying that you’ve found the upper limit of God’s designing/creating capabilities.

"No, I can tell you, not even God would be able to devise a set of fundamental laws from which planets would form, and in some of them, or one of them, humans would evolve. I won’t even deal with something as complex as the brain, you can just stop with the vertebrate’s eye. Absolutely beyond God’s capabilities to anticipate, for sure, that it would evolve. He’d have to hand-craft it manually, that’s the only way he would manage to do it. It’s like Paley’s analogy with the watchmaker. Organisms are complex, like watches, that don’t assemble themselves spontaneously. Therefore a designer is required. But you can’t use the same logic and propose a designed watchmaking system. The watches have to be hand-crafted, not produced themselves by an automatic invention. Even God’s creativity has its limits, just like he can’t create a stone so heavy that he can’t lift."
